Background on Dalit Literature


In the late 20th century, Dalit literature emerged as a distinct political literary critique, countering mainstream literary trends. Through their writing, Dalit authors conceive a broader political community and freedoms centered around new universal ideals of humanity and human dignity, encapsulated in the Marathi word manuski (Paik 2022). Through a wide range of fictional and non-fictional writing in Indian language public spheres, Dalit authors have asked and attempted to answer what exactly defines Dalit literature. They have also raised critical caste consciousness through their literary organizing. As such, Dalit authors contribute to contemporary political debates, illustrating the inextricable connection between Dalit literature and Dalit political activism and mobilization.


Across the four languages, Dalit literary organizing has been inseparable from political organizing. Through their writing, Dalit authors have envisioned and formed respective Dalit political publics. As one of the earliest centers of Dalit activism, the Marathi public sphere has shaped how Dalit authors engage with political organizing. Marathi Dalit literary critique flourished in the 1960s–1970s, reflecting the influence of Bhimrao Ramji Ambedkar (1891–1956) and with the emergence of the Dalit Panthers in 1972.


As a paradigmatic case, the Marathi Dalit literary movement has shaped how Dalit authors have mobilized through other languages, including Hindu/Urdu, Bengali, and Tamil. This type of organizing was adopted by other regions with the establishment of local Ambedkarite political organizations, Dalit literary organizations, Dalit literary conferences, and the publication of periodicals or magazines in vernacular languages.
