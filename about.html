Background on Dalit Literature

In the late 20th century, Dalit literature emerged as a distinct political literary critique, countering mainstream literary trends. Through their writing, authors from Dalit (caste-oppressed) communities have conceived a broader political community and freedoms centered around expansive understandings of humanity and human dignity, encapsulated in the Marathi word manuski (Paik 2022). Through a wide range of fiction and non-fiction writing in South Asian language public spheres, Dalit authors have raised critical caste consciousness through their literary organizing. As such, Dalit authors contribute to contemporary political debates, illustrating the inextricable connection between Dalit literature and Dalit political activism and mobilization.


As one of the earliest centers of Dalit activism, the Marathi public sphere has shaped how Dalit authors engage with political organizing. Marathi Dalit literary critique flourished in the 1960s–1970s, reflecting the influence of Bhimrao Ramji Ambedkar (1891–1956) and with the emergence of the Dalit Panthers in 1972. As a paradigmatic case, the Marathi Dalit literary movement has shaped how Dalit authors have mobilized through other languages, including Hindu/Urdu, Bengali, and Tamil. This type of organizing was adopted by other regions with the establishment of local Ambedkarite political organizations, Dalit literary organizations, Dalit literary conferences, and the publication of periodicals or magazines in vernacular languages. Through their writing, Dalit authors have envisioned and formed respective Dalit political publics.
